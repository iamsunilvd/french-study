Third Person -s rules
Most verbs: add -s → works, plays, runs
Ends in -s,-ss,-sh,-ch,-x,-z: add -es → watches, washes, fixes
Consonant + y: -y → -ies → studies, tries, carries
Vowel + y: add -s → plays, buys, says
Irregulars: has (have) · does (do) · goes (go) · is (be)
Signal words: always, usually, often, sometimes, never, every day/week/year, on Mondays, in general.

▾

-ing spelling rules
Most verbs: add -ing → working, playing, reading
Ends in -e: drop e + -ing → making, writing, coming, having
Short CVC (consonant-vowel-consonant): double final consonant → running, sitting, swimming, beginning

Ends in -y: just add -ing → playing, studying

Past Simple
Completed actions at a specific past time
▾
Affirmative — regular verb: to work
ALL personsworked (same form for all)
Affirmative — irregular verb: to go
ALL personswent (must memorize irregular forms)
Negative — ALL persons same
ALL personsdid not (didn't) work / go
Question
ALL personsDid I/you/he/she/we/they work/go?
Regular -ed spelling rules
Most verbs: add -ed → worked, played, opened
Ends in -e: add -d → loved, hoped, used, liked
Short CVC: double final consonant → stopped, planned, dropped, hugged
Consonant + y: -y → -ied → tried, studied, carried, replied
Vowel + y: add -ed → played, enjoyed, stayed
Signal words: yesterday, last week/month/year, ago, in 2010, when I was young, then, at that time, in those days.

Future Simple (will)
Decisions · Promises · Predictions · Facts about future
▾

Going to — planned future

Modal Verbs — Full Reference
can · could · may · might · must · shall · should · will · would
✦
Modal Verbs — Grammar Rules
How ALL modals work structurally
modal
▾
Structure Rules (apply to ALL modals)
1. No -s for he/she/it: She cans → She can
2. Followed by base verb (no "to"): He must to go → He must go
3. No do/does for questions: Do you can swim? → Can you swim?
4. No do/does for negatives: He doesn't can → He can't / He cannot
5. No infinitive, no past participle: modals never change form
6. No continuous form: He is musting → ✗ impossible
Modal + have + past participle (perfect modals)
Used to speculate about the past or express regret:
She must have left. (deduction about past)
He could have called. (past possibility — didn't happen)
They should have studied. (regret/criticism about past)
You might have told me! (mild reproach)
I would have helped. (unrealized past intention)
①
can / cannot / can't
Ability · Informal permission · Possibility · Request
modal
▾
All forms — ALL persons same
affirmativecan do / can swim / can help
negativecannot / can't do
questionCan I/you/he/she/we/they do?
Uses with examples
Ability: She can speak five languages.
Informal permission: Can I borrow your pen?
Request: Can you help me?
Possibility: That can't be true!
Past ability: use could: I could run fast when I was young.
Future ability: use will be able to: I will be able to attend.
No past form — use "could" for past ability. No future form — use "will be able to". Perfect: "could have done."
②
could / couldn't
Past ability · Polite requests · Possibility · Conditional
modal
▾
Uses with examples
Past ability: I could swim when I was 5.
Polite request (more formal than can): Could you open the window, please?
Possibility (present/future, uncertain): It could rain tomorrow.
Conditional (hypothetical): If I had more time, I could help.
Suggestion: You could try talking to her.
Regret about past (could have): I could have called but I forgot.
③
may / might
Possibility · Formal permission · Uncertainty
modal
▾
may — uses
Formal permission: You may leave now. (more formal than "can")
Possibility (fairly likely): It may rain this afternoon.
Wishes (formal/literary): May you have a wonderful life!
Perfect (past possibility): She may have left already.
might — uses
Weaker possibility (less certain than may): I might come to the party. (I'm not sure)
Conditional (more tentative): It might be a good idea.
Mild suggestion: You might want to reconsider.
Perfect: He might have forgotten. (past possibility)

may = ~50% certain · might = ~30% certain · could = possible but less probable
④
must / must not (mustn't)
Strong obligation · Logical deduction · Prohibition
modal
▾
must — positive uses
Strong internal obligation: I must finish this tonight.
Strong recommendation: You must try this restaurant!
Logical deduction (certainty): She must be tired — she worked all day.
Perfect deduction: He must have left early.
must not (mustn't) — prohibition
Prohibition (it is forbidden): You mustn't smoke in here.
Negative deduction: That can't / couldn't be right. (NOT "mustn't be")

⚠️ must not ≠ don't have to
You mustn't go = it's forbidden
You don't have to go = it's not necessary (but you can if you want)
No past form — use "had to" for past obligation. No future form — use "will have to". Present perfect: "must have done."
⑤
shall / should / ought to
Advice · Mild obligation · Expectation · Offers (British)
modal
▾
shall
Primarily British English. Used for:
Offers: Shall I open the window?
Suggestions: Shall we go out for dinner?
Future (formal/first person): We shall overcome.
should
Advice/recommendation: You should see a doctor.
Mild obligation: You should be on time.
Expectation (things that ought to happen): The train should arrive at 9.
Conditional (formal): Should you need help, call me.
Regret (should have): I should have studied harder.
ought to
Similar meaning to "should" but slightly stronger moral obligation:
You ought to apologise.
He ought to be there by now.
Negative: ought not to / oughtn't to (quite formal — "shouldn't" is more common)
⑥
will / would
Future · Conditional · Polite requests · Past habits
modal
▾
will — uses
Spontaneous decision: I'll have the pasta, please.
Promise: I'll call you tonight.
Prediction (no evidence): I think it will rain.
Offer: I'll carry that for you.
Stubborn refusal (won't): He won't listen!
would — uses
Conditional (2nd/3rd): I would help if I could.
Polite request (more formal than will): Would you pass the salt?
Polite offer/invitation: Would you like some tea?
Past habits (repeated past actions): When I was young, we would go to the beach every summer.
Reported speech future: She said she would call.
Preference (would rather / would prefer): I'd rather stay home.
⑦
Semi-Modals
have to · need to · used to · be able to · be going to
modal
▾
have to — external obligation
Conjugates like a regular verb (DO/DOES/DID):
I have to go. She has to work. They had to leave.
Do you have to wear a uniform?
I will have to study harder. (future obligation — unlike "must")
need to — necessity
You need to see a doctor. (regular verb construction)
She doesn't need to come. (not necessary)
Also modal: Need I say more? (formal/literary)
used to — repeated past actions/states
Always refers to past — something no longer true:
I used to smoke. (I don't now)
She used to live in Paris.
Neg: I didn't use to like it.
Question: Did you use to go there?

≠ I'm used to + -ing (accustomed to): I'm used to waking up early.
≠ I got used to + -ing (became accustomed): I got used to the noise.
be able to — ability (all tenses)
Substitutes for "can" in tenses where "can" has no form:
I will be able to help. (future)
She was able to escape. (past — specific achievement)
I've never been able to swim. (perfect)

Auxiliary Verbs in Depth
be · have · do — as auxiliaries and main verbs
①
DO as Auxiliary
Questions · Negatives · Emphasis · Tag questions
auxiliary
▾
Forming Questions (Present & Past Simple)
Present: Do/Does + subject + base verb?
Do you like coffee? Does she work here?

Past: Did + subject + base verb?
Did you see that? Did he call?
Forming Negatives
Present: don't / doesn't + base verb
I don't know. She doesn't care.

Past: didn't + base verb
I didn't see. They didn't come.
Emphatic DO
Adds emphasis — stressed in speech:
I DO like it! (I really like it)
She DID tell you! (contradicting someone)
Do come in! (emphatic invitation)
Tag Questions
Positive statement → negative tag: You like it, don't you?
Negative statement → positive tag: You don't like it, do you?
With other auxiliaries: She's coming, isn't she? / He can swim, can't he? / They've finished, haven't they?
②
BE as Auxiliary
Continuous tenses · Passive voice
auxiliary
▾
BE for Continuous Tenses
be + verb-ing
I am working. (present continuous)
She was running. (past continuous)
They will be waiting. (future continuous)
He has been trying. (present perfect continuous)
BE for Passive Voice
be (in any tense) + past participle
The letter is written. (present simple passive)
The letter was written. (past simple passive)
The letter has been written. (present perfect passive)
The letter will be written. (future passive)
The letter should be written. (modal passive)
③
HAVE as Auxiliary
All perfect tenses
auxiliary
▾
HAVE for Perfect Tenses
have/has + past participle → present perfect
I have finished. She has gone.

had + past participle → past perfect
I had finished before she arrived.

will have + past participle → future perfect
I will have finished by 5pm.

have/has been + -ing → present perfect continuous
I've been waiting for an hour.

had been + -ing → past perfect continuous
She had been working all night.

Infinitive Forms
To-infinitive vs bare infinitive
▾
To-infinitive — use after
Many verbs: want, need, hope, decide, plan, agree, refuse, manage, fail, afford, offer, expect, seem, appear, tend, happen
I want to go. She decided to stay. He seems to know.

Adjectives: It's good to see you. I'm happy to help.
Purpose: I came here to study.
After nouns: I have no time to waste.
Bare infinitive (no "to") — use after
Modal verbs: You must go. She can swim.
let / make / have (causative): Let him speak. Make her wait. Have them sign it.
Perception verbs (see/hear/feel + object): I saw him leave. I heard her sing.
help (both possible): Help me (to) find it.
had better / would rather: You'd better go. I'd rather stay.

Gerund (-ing as noun)
As subject, object, after prepositions, after certain verbs
▾
Gerund as subject
Swimming is good exercise.
Learning English takes time.
Smoking is bad for your health.
Gerund after prepositions (ALWAYS -ing after prepositions)
I'm interested in learning more.
She's good at cooking.
He's afraid of flying.
I'm looking forward to seeing you.
Before leaving, check everything.
After finishing work, she went home.
Verbs followed by gerund
enjoy, avoid, finish, mind, suggest, consider, keep, deny, miss, admit, practise, risk, fancy, involve, imagine, recommend, regret (past), stop (quit), remember (past), try (experiment), like/love/hate (BrE)

I enjoy reading. She avoided answering. He kept talking.

Causative Structures
have/get something done · make/let someone do
▾
have/get + object + past participle
Someone else does something for you (arranged/caused):
I had my car repaired. (= someone repaired my car)
She got her hair cut.
I'll have the report sent to you.
We got the house painted last summer.

Also used for negative events: I had my wallet stolen.
make/have + object + base verb
Force or arrange someone to do something:
The teacher made us rewrite the essay. (forced)
She had her assistant prepare the documents. (arranged)
Passive form: We were made to wait. (add "to" in passive)
let + object + base verb
Allow someone to do something:
Let me help you.
They let us leave early.
My parents let me stay out late.
get + object + to-infinitive
Persuade/convince someone:
I got him to agree.
She got her boss to approve the budget.
⑤
Imperative
Commands · Instructions · Requests · Warnings
▾
Formation
Positive: base verb (no subject)
Sit down. Open your book. Be quiet. Listen!

Negative: Don't + base verb
Don't worry. Don't be late. Don't touch that!

Inclusive (we): Let's + base verb
Let's go! Let's have a break. Let's not argue.

Emphatic (you specifically): You + base verb
You stay here! You be quiet!

Polite: add "please" before or after:
Please sit down. / Sit down, please.

Stative Verbs
Verbs that rarely use continuous forms
▾
Categories of stative verbs
Mental states: know, believe, understand, think (opinion), remember, forget, recognise, mean, realise, suppose, imagine

Emotions: love, like, hate, prefer, want, wish, need, fear, envy, care

Senses: see, hear, smell, taste, feel (when involuntary)

Possession: have (possession), own, belong, possess, contain

Other: seem, appear, look (seem), depend, consist, include, involve, matter, owe, cost, weigh, measure
Verbs with BOTH stative and dynamic uses
think: I think it's great. (opinion) vs I'm thinking about it. (active process)
have: I have a car. (possession) vs I'm having lunch. (activity)
see: I see the problem. (understand) vs I'm seeing a doctor. (appointment)
smell: It smells good. (stative) vs He's smelling the flowers. (action)
taste: It tastes sweet. (stative) vs She's tasting the soup. (action)

Essential Phrasal Verbs
150+ most common — separable & inseparable
▾
Separable vs Inseparable
Separable: object can go between verb and particle:
Turn off the TV. / Turn the TV off.
(If object is pronoun, MUST separate: Turn it off. ✓ / Turn off it. ✗)

Inseparable: object always comes after the particle:
Look after the children. (not: ✗ look the children after)
