A vs An — Indefinite Articles
When to use "a" or "an"
Beginner
▾
What it is

Indefinite articles introduce a non-specific noun. The choice between a and an depends entirely on sound, not spelling.

Rule
Use an before a vowel sound (a, e, i, o, u sounds).
Use a before a consonant sound.

Key: it's about the pronunciation of the next word, not its first letter.
Examples
vowel sound → an apple, an hour, an honest man, an MBA
consonant sound → a university, a European, a one-way street, a useful tool
Common Mistakes
✗a hour → an hour (h is silent — sounds like "our")
✗an university → a university (sounds like "you-niversity")
✗an European → a European (sounds like "yoo-ropean")
Tip
When in doubt, say the phrase out loud. If your mouth opens naturally for the next sound, use "an".
2
The — Definite Article
When to use and when NOT to use "the"
Intermediate
▾
What it is

"The" refers to a specific noun that both speaker and listener know about. This is one of the most common errors made by non-native speakers.

When to USE "the"
1. Specific thing both parties know: Close the door.
2. Unique things: the sun, the moon, the president
3. Second mention of something: I saw a dog. The dog was big.
4. Superlatives: the best, the fastest
5. Rivers, oceans, mountain ranges: the Thames, the Alps
When NOT to use "the"
1. General statements: Dogs are friendly. (not "The dogs")
2. Most countries, cities: France, London (but: the USA, the UK)
3. Languages: She speaks French.
4. Meals, sports: I had breakfast. He plays football.
5. Academic subjects: She studies History.
Common Mistakes
✗The life is beautiful. → Life is beautiful. (general)
✗I love the nature. → I love nature. (general concept)
✗She went to the school. → She went to school. (as a function)

Countable vs Uncountable Nouns
much / many / some / any / a few / a little
Intermediate
▾
What it is

Countable nouns can be counted (one book, two books). Uncountable nouns cannot (water, advice, information). This determines which quantifiers you use.

Quantifier Rules
QuantifierCountableUncountable
many / a few / few✓ many books✗
much / a little / little✗✓ much water
some / any / a lot of / plenty of✓ both✓ both
a / an✓ a book✗ (a water ✗)
a piece of / a bit of✗✓ a piece of advice
Common Mistakes
✗I have many informations. → I have a lot of information.
✗Can you give me an advice? → Can you give me some advice?
✗How much books do you have? → How many books do you have?
Tip
Common uncountables to memorize: advice, information, news, furniture, luggage, equipment, knowledge, research, traffic, weather, money (but "coins" is countable).

5
Simple Present
Habits, facts, routines, general truths
Beginner
▾
Form & Usage
Form: base verb (+ -s/-es for he/she/it)
Uses:
1. Habits/routines: She walks to work every day.
2. General truths/facts: The Earth orbits the Sun.
3. Permanent situations: He lives in Paris.
4. Timetables (future): The train leaves at 9am.
Examples
I drink coffee every morning.
She doesn't eat meat.
Do they live nearby?
Water boils at 100°C.
Common Mistakes
✗She work hard. → She works hard. (add -s for he/she/it)
✗He don't know. → He doesn't know.
6
Present Continuous
Actions happening now or temporarily
Beginner
▾
Form & Usage
Form: am/is/are + verb-ing
Uses:
1. Right now: I'm writing an email.
2. Temporary situations: She's staying with friends.
3. Changing trends: Prices are rising.
4. Planned future: We're meeting tomorrow.
Stative Verbs — DO NOT use with -ing
Some verbs describe states, not actions, and almost never use continuous form:
know, believe, want, need, love, hate, like, understand, remember, seem, belong, contain, mean, prefer, own

I am knowing the answer. → I know the answer.
Common Mistakes
✗I am understanding you. → I understand you.
✗She is seeming tired. → She seems tired.
7
Simple Past vs Present Perfect
The most confusing tense distinction in English
Intermediate
▾
The Key Difference

Simple Past = a completed action at a specific time in the past.
Present Perfect = a past action with a connection to the present (result, experience, or unfinished time).

Simple Past — use when
Time is specified or implied as finished:
I visited Paris in 2019.
She left an hour ago.
Did you see the news yesterday?
Present Perfect — use when
Time is unspecified or the period isn't over:
I have visited Paris. (life experience)
She has just left. (result: she's not here)
Have you seen the news today? (today isn't finished)
Key words: just, already, yet, ever, never, recently, so far, this week/month/year
Common Mistakes
✗I have seen him yesterday. → I saw him yesterday. (specific past time)
✗Did you ever try sushi? → Have you ever tried sushi? (life experience)
✗I lost my keys. Did you see them? → Have you seen them? (still relevant now)
8
Past Continuous
Actions in progress at a past moment
Intermediate
▾
Form & Usage
Form: was/were + verb-ing
1. In progress at a specific past time: At 8pm I was cooking dinner.
2. Interrupted by another action: I was sleeping when the phone rang.
3. Parallel actions: While she was reading, he was cooking.
4. Setting background scene: It was raining and the streets were empty.
Past Continuous vs Simple Past
I was walking home when I met her. (was walking = longer action; met = interruption)
I walked home and met her. (two sequential completed actions)
9
Past Perfect
The "further past" — before another past event
Intermediate
▾
Form & Usage
Form: had + past participle
Use when you need to show that one past event happened before another past event.

When I arrived, the film had already started.
She hadn't eaten anything, so she was hungry.
He recognised her because he had seen her before.
Tip
Think of it as "the past of the past." If you're already speaking in past tense and need to go back even further — use past perfect.
Common Mistakes
✗When I arrived, the film started already. → the film had already started.
10
Future Forms
will vs going to vs present continuous
Intermediate
▾
Will — use for
1. Spontaneous decisions (decided now): I'll have the soup, please.
2. Predictions (no evidence): I think it will rain tomorrow.
3. Promises/offers/requests: I'll help you. Will you hold this?
4. Facts about the future: She'll be 30 next year.
Going to — use for
1. Plans already decided: I'm going to visit my parents next week.
2. Predictions with evidence: Look at those clouds — it's going to rain.
Present Continuous — use for
Fixed arrangements with time/place: I'm meeting John at 3pm tomorrow.
(There's a firm plan/appointment)
Common Mistakes
✗I will go to the dentist tomorrow. (if already booked) → I'm going to / I'm going to see the dentist.
✗Look! It will rain! (visible evidence) → It's going to rain!
11
Perfect Continuous Tenses
have/had been doing — duration + result
Advanced
▾
Present Perfect Continuous
Form: have/has been + verb-ing
Emphasises duration or the activity itself (ongoing or recently stopped):
I've been working here for 10 years. (still working)
You look tired. Have you been running? (evidence of recent activity)
Present Perfect vs Present Perfect Continuous
I've read three chapters. → focus on result/completion
I've been reading all afternoon. → focus on duration/activity

She has written the report. → it's done ✓
She has been writing the report. → she's been busy (may not be done)
Past Perfect Continuous
Form: had been + verb-ing
Duration up to a past point:
She had been waiting for two hours when he arrived.
His eyes were red because he had been crying.

13
Must vs Have To vs Should
Degrees of obligation
Intermediate
▾
The Differences
must = internal obligation (I decide it's necessary) OR strong deduction
I must finish this today. (I feel it's essential)
She must be home — the lights are on. (deduction)

have to = external obligation (rules, laws, other people)
I have to wear a seatbelt. (it's the law)
Do you have to work weekends?

should = advice, recommendation, mild obligation
You should drink more water.
The train should arrive soon. (expectation)
Negatives change meaning dramatically
must not = it is forbidden: You must not smoke here.
don't have to = it is NOT necessary: You don't have to come.
shouldn't = it is not advisable: You shouldn't eat so fast.
14
Passive Voice
Active vs passive — when and how to use it
Intermediate
▾
What it is

In the passive, the object of the action becomes the subject. Use passive when the agent is unknown, unimportant, or when you want to emphasize the action or result over who did it.

Form
Subject + be (conjugated) + past participle (+ by agent)

Active: The chef cooked the meal.
Passive: The meal was cooked (by the chef).
Passive Across Tenses
TenseActivePassive
Present simpleThey make cars here.Cars are made here.
Past simpleHe broke the window.The window was broken.
Present perfectThey have arrested him.He has been arrested.
FutureThey will build a bridge.A bridge will be built.
ModalYou must sign this.This must be signed.
Tip
Use passive in formal/academic writing when the agent is irrelevant: "The experiment was conducted over three months." Avoid overusing it in everyday speech — it can sound unnatural.

Intermediate
▾
The Five Conditionals
TypeMeaningFormExample
ZeroGeneral truth/always trueIf + present, presentIf you heat ice, it melts.
FirstReal/likely futureIf + present, willIf it rains, I'll stay home.
SecondUnreal/unlikely present or futureIf + past, wouldIf I had more money, I would travel.

✗I wish I would be taller. → I wish I were taller.

Gerunds & Infinitives
enjoy doing vs want to do — verb patterns
Intermediate
▾
Verbs followed by GERUND (-ing)
enjoy, avoid, finish, mind, suggest, consider, keep, deny, miss, admit, practise, risk, fancy, involve

I enjoy swimming.

Verbs followed by INFINITIVE (to + verb)
want, need, decide, hope, plan, expect, agree, refuse, manage, fail, afford, offer, promise, tend

I want to leave.

try + -ing = experiment: Try adding lemon.
try + to = attempt: Try to be on time.

Subject-Verb Agreement
The verb must match the subject
Beginner
▾
Core Rules
1. Singular subject → singular verb: She runs every day.
2. Plural subject → plural verb: They run every day.
3. Compound with "and" → plural: Tom and Lisa are here.
4. "or / nor" → agree with nearest noun: Neither the students nor the teacher was there.
Tricky Cases
Collective nouns (team, family, government) — singular in American English, can be singular or plural in British:
The team is ready. (AmE) / The team are ready. (BrE)

Everyone, someone, nobody → singular:
Everyone has a ticket.

None of → usually singular but plural is becoming accepted:
None of the water is left. / None of the students were absent.
Common Mistakes
✗The news are bad. → The news is bad. (news is singular)
✗The number of students are increasing. → The number … is increasing.
✗Each of the boys have a pen. → Each of the boys has a pen.

Relative Clauses
who / which / that / where / whose — defining vs non-defining
Intermediate
▾
Which pronoun to use
PronounRefers toExample
whopeopleThe man who called is waiting.
whichthings / animalsThe book which you lent me was great.
thatpeople or things (defining only)The car that I bought is blue.
whereplacesThe town where I grew up was small.
whosepossessionThe woman whose bag was stolen called police.
Defining vs Non-Defining
Defining (no commas) — essential info, identifies the noun:
The student who passed the exam got a prize.

Non-defining (commas required) — extra info, noun already identified:
My sister, who lives in London, is a doctor.

⚠️ "that" cannot be used in non-defining clauses:
My sister, that lives in London… → My sister, who lives in London…
20
Conjunctions
Connecting ideas — coordinating & subordinating
Beginner
▾
Coordinating (FANBOYS)
For · And · Nor · But · Or · Yet · So
Join two independent clauses (equal weight):
I was tired, but I finished the work.
She studied hard, so she passed.
Subordinating Conjunctions
Introduce a dependent clause. Key examples:
because, since, as (reason): I left because I was tired.
although, even though, though, while (contrast): Although it was raining, we went out.
if, unless, provided that (condition): Unless you hurry, you'll be late.
when, while, before, after, until, as soon as (time)
Common Mistakes
✗Although he was tired, but he kept working. → Although he was tired, he kept working. (don't use both although + but)
✗Because of he was late. → Because he was late. / Because of his lateness.

22
Inversion
Inverted word order for emphasis — formal & literary
Advanced
▾
What it is

Inversion reverses the normal subject-verb order for dramatic emphasis, usually in formal or literary English. Common after negative adverbials.

After Negative Adverbials
Never have I seen such chaos. (= I have never seen…)
Rarely does she make mistakes.
Not only did he lie, but he also stole.
Scarcely had I sat down when the phone rang.
No sooner had he left than it started raining.
Hardly had she arrived when the trouble began.
Other Inversion Contexts
Conditionals without "if":
Were I you, I wouldn't go. (= If I were you)
Had I known, I would have helped. (= If I had known)
Should you need help, call me. (= If you should need)
23
Cleft Sentences
It is… that / What… is — emphasis through splitting
Advanced
▾
What it is

A cleft sentence splits one clause into two to put extra focus on one element. Very useful in writing and formal speech.

It-Clefts
Structure: It is/was + [focus] + that/who…

Normal: John broke the window.
Cleft (focus on John): It was John who broke the window.
Cleft (focus on window): It was the window that John broke.
Cleft (focus on time): It was yesterday that he called.
Wh-Clefts (Pseudo-Clefts)
Structure: What + [clause] + is/was + [focus]

What I need is a holiday.
What surprised me was his reaction.
What she wants is to be left alone.

Apostrophes
Possession and contractions — the most misused mark
Beginner
▾
Possession
Singular noun → 's: the dog's bone, James's car
Plural noun ending in -s → s': the dogs' bone, the teachers' room
Irregular plural → 's: the children's toys, the women's team
Contractions
it's = it is / it has: It's raining. It's been a long day.
its = possessive: The cat licked its paw.
you're = you are | your = possessive
they're = they are | their = possessive | there = place
who's = who is/has | whose = possessive
Common Mistakes
✗The dog hurt it's leg. → its leg (possessive, no apostrophe)
✗I love pizza's and pasta's. → pizzas and pastas (no apostrophe for plurals!)
✗Your welcome. → You're welcome.
25
Commas
When to use — and when NOT to use — a comma
Intermediate
▾
When to USE a comma
1. Lists: I bought milk, eggs, bread, and butter.
2. Before coordinating conjunctions (joining two independent clauses): I was tired, but I stayed.
3. After introductory phrases: However, I disagree. In 2020, everything changed.
4. Non-defining relative clauses: My brother, who lives abroad, called me.
5. Direct address: Come in, John. Let's eat, Grandma.
Comma Splice — a serious error
✗I came home, I made dinner, I went to bed.
→ I came home, made dinner, and went to bed.
→ or: I came home; I made dinner; I went to bed.
Oxford Comma
The Oxford comma is the final comma before "and" in a list. I love my parents, Lady Gaga, and Bono (with Oxford comma — three separate things). Without it: I love my parents, Lady Gaga and Bono — ambiguous!
26
Semicolons, Colons & Dashes
The most underused — and misused — marks
Advanced
▾
Semicolon ( ; )
Joins two closely related independent clauses without a conjunction:
I love coffee; I can't function without it.
She studied hard; her results showed it.

Also used in complex lists:
Attendees came from Paris, France; Rome, Italy; and Madrid, Spain.
Colon ( : )
Introduces what follows — a list, explanation, or quotation:
I need three things: time, money, and patience.
The problem was clear: nobody had planned ahead.

⚠️ What comes before the colon must be a complete sentence.
Em Dash ( — ) vs Hyphen ( - )
Em dash — used for interruption, emphasis, or parenthetical info:
The answer — surprisingly — was yes.
She had one goal — to win.

Hyphen - used to join compound modifiers before a noun:
a well-known author, a five-year-old child, an up-to-date report
But: The author is well known. (no hyphen after the noun)

Commonly Confused Words
affect/effect · fewer/less · lie/lay · bring/take
Intermediate
▾
affect vs effect
affect = verb (to have an impact on): Stress affects your health.
effect = noun (the result): The effect of stress on health is serious.
Memory trick: RAVEN — Remember Affect Verb Effect Noun
fewer vs less
fewer = countable nouns: fewer people, fewer cars, fewer mistakes
less = uncountable nouns: less water, less time, less money

Supermarket sign: "10 items or less" → should be fewer!
lie vs lay
lie (intransitive) = to recline; no object needed
Present: I lie down. Past: I lay down. Past participle: lain

lay (transitive) = to put/place; needs an object
Present: I lay the book on the table. Past: I laid it down. PP: laid
bring vs take
bring = movement toward speaker/listener: Bring me a coffee. I'll bring wine to the party.
take = movement away from speaker/listener: Take this letter to the post office.
28
More Confusing Pairs
who/whom · that/which · among/between · good/well
Intermediate
▾
who vs whom
who = subject (doing the action)
whom = object (receiving the action)
Trick: if you can answer with "him/her" → use whom. If "he/she" → who.

Who called? → He called. ✓
Whom did you call? → I called him. ✓
good vs well
good = adjective (describes a noun): She's a good writer.
well = adverb (describes a verb): She writes well.

⚠️ I'm good (AmE, informal, describes how I am) vs I'm well (refers to health)
between vs among
between = two things (or distinct individual items): between you and me; between the three candidates
among = three or more things seen as a group: among the crowd, among friends
further vs farther
farther = physical distance: London is farther than Oxford.
further = figurative/metaphorical distance, or "additional": further research, further notice
(In British English, "further" is often used for both.)

The Subjunctive Mood
Hypothetical, wishes, demands, recommendations
Advanced
▾
What it is

The subjunctive expresses hypothetical situations, wishes, demands, and recommendations. It's more common in formal/written English and American English than in casual British speech.

Present Subjunctive (base form)
Used after verbs of suggestion, recommendation, demand, necessity:
suggest, recommend, demand, insist, propose, request, require

I suggest that he come early. (not "comes")
The doctor recommended that she take rest.
It is essential that every student be present.
They demanded that he resign.
Past Subjunctive (were)
Used for hypothetical/unreal conditions and wishes. Use were for all persons (not "was"):

If I were you, I wouldn't say that.
I wish I were taller.
If she were here, she would know what to do.
Suppose he were to find out…
Tip
"If I was" is increasingly used in informal speech, but "If I were" remains preferred in formal writing and exams.
32
Participle Clauses
Compressed clauses using -ing, -ed, or having done
Advanced
▾
What it is

Participle clauses replace full clauses, making writing more concise and formal. The subject of the participle clause must be the same as the main clause subject.

Present Participle (-ing)
Simultaneous or immediately preceding action:
Full: Because she felt ill, she left early.
Reduced: Feeling ill, she left early.

Walking down the street, I noticed a poster.
Not knowing what to do, he asked for help.
Past Participle (-ed)
Usually passive meaning:
Full: The report, which was written last year, is still relevant.
Reduced: Written last year, the report is still relevant.

Shocked by the news, she sat down.
Exhausted after the race, he collapsed.
Perfect Participle (having done)
Action completed before the main clause:
Having finished her work, she went home.
Having been told the news, he couldn't sleep.
Dangling Participles — a serious error
✗Walking home, the rain started. (the rain wasn't walking!)
→ Walking home, I got caught in the rain.
33
Ellipsis & Substitution
Avoiding repetition elegantly
Advanced
▾
Ellipsis (omitting words)
Leaving out words that can be understood from context:

A: Can you come tomorrow? B: I think [I can come tomorrow] so. → I think so.
She can play piano and [she can play] guitar.
I'd like to come but [I] can't [come].
Substitution with "do so" / "one"
do so replaces a verb phrase:
He promised to call, and he did so that evening.

one / ones replaces a noun:
I need a pen. Do you have one?
I prefer the red shoes to the black ones.
34
Nominalization
Turning verbs/adjectives into nouns for formal writing
Advanced
▾
What it is

Nominalization converts verbs or adjectives into noun phrases. Common in academic, legal, and formal writing. Used strategically, it adds precision; overused, it creates dense, unreadable prose.

Examples
Verb/AdjectiveNominalized Form
decide → decision / the decision to…
investigate → investigation
fail → failure

significant → significance

develop → development
In practice
Simple: We decided to invest in the project.
Nominalized: The decision to invest in the project was unanimous.
Tip
Nominalization allows the action to become the topic. Use it when you want to discuss an event or concept as a thing itself, not just describe what happened.

-ing vs -ed Adjectives
boring/bored · confusing/confused · interesting/interested
Intermediate
▾
The Rule
-ing adjectives describe the cause or characteristic:
The film is boring. (the film causes boredom)
The instructions are confusing.

-ed adjectives describe the effect on a person (how they feel):
I am bored. (I feel boredom)
I am confused by the instructions.
